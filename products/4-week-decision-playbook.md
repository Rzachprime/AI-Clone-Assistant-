# 4-Week Decision Playbook

## Purpose

This playbook helps clients strengthen how they make decisions with the support of a personal AI clone.

It is not a guide for making one perfect decision.

It is a structured month of practice for becoming clearer, steadier, and more effective in the way decisions are made.

The clone is used as a support system for:

- reflection
- framing
- tradeoff analysis
- pattern recognition
- follow-through

## Why a Decision Playbook Matters

Many people do not struggle because they cannot think.

They struggle because decisions get distorted by:

- overthinking
- ambiguity
- fear of consequence
- unclear priorities
- emotional noise
- lack of follow-through

Over time, this creates a pattern:

- delay
- drift
- avoidance
- reactive choices

This playbook is designed to interrupt that pattern.

## The Core Principle

Better decisions rarely come from more pressure.

They usually come from:

- clearer framing
- stronger self-awareness
- more honest tradeoffs
- more consistent follow-through

That is the work of this playbook.

## What This Playbook Is For

This playbook is designed for people who want to:

- make decisions with more clarity
- stop circling the same unresolved questions
- reduce indecision
- feel more honest about tradeoffs
- build a better decision rhythm

## What This Playbook Is Not For

This playbook is not designed to:

- replace legal, financial, or medical expertise
- eliminate uncertainty entirely
- make high-stakes decisions automatic
- remove responsibility from the user

The clone supports the decision process.

The user still owns the decision.

## How to Use This Playbook

Each week has:

- a weekly focus
- why it matters
- one or more AI prompts
- a practical task
- what to look for

The goal is not to do everything perfectly.

The goal is to create a month of stronger decision-making habits.

## Before You Begin

Choose one real decision area to work with during the month.

Examples:

- work priorities
- an unresolved personal decision
- a role or project choice
- a time or energy allocation problem
- a recurring pattern of indecision

The playbook will be more useful if it is grounded in something real.

## Week 1: Clarify the Decision Landscape

## Why This Week Matters

Many decision problems are not really decision problems.

They are clarity problems.

People often:

- combine multiple decisions into one
- confuse fear with fact
- chase certainty before defining the choice

The goal of Week 1 is to see the decision landscape clearly.

## What to Do

Use the clone to identify:

- what decisions are actually in front of you
- which ones matter most
- which ones are being avoided
- where confusion is coming from

## Prompt 1: Decision Landscape

```text
Act as my decision support assistant.

Help me map the decision landscape I am dealing with right now.

Please identify:
- the decisions that are actually present
- which ones are most important
- which ones I may be mixing together
- where I seem unclear, avoidant, or overloaded

Keep the answer simple, structured, and honest.
```

## Prompt 2: What Is the Real Decision?

```text
Act as my decision framing assistant.

Based on the notes below, help me identify what the real decision is.

Please tell me:
- what I am actually choosing between
- what is not yet a decision, but only a concern or fear
- what smaller decision may need to happen first
```

## Practical Task

Write down:

- the top three decisions currently taking mental space
- which one matters most this month
- which one you may have been avoiding

## What to Look For

By the end of Week 1, you should be able to say:

- what the decision really is
- what it is not
- what feels muddy
- where more clarity is needed

## Week 2: Surface Tradeoffs and Friction

## Why This Week Matters

A decision becomes easier when the real tradeoffs are visible.

Many people stay stuck because they are trying to avoid:

- loss
- discomfort
- regret
- identity tension

The goal of Week 2 is not to remove tradeoffs.

It is to see them more honestly.

## What to Do

Use the clone to identify:

- what each option gives
- what each option costs
- what fear may be distorting judgment
- what values are in tension

## Prompt 1: Tradeoff Analysis

```text
Act as my decision support assistant.

Help me see the tradeoffs in this decision more clearly.

Please show:
- what each option offers
- what each option costs
- what I may be trying to avoid
- what value or priority seems to be in tension

Do not force a conclusion. Help me see the structure of the choice.
```

## Prompt 2: Friction and Resistance

```text
Act as my reflection assistant.

Help me identify what is creating resistance around this decision.

Please show:
- what I may be afraid of
- what loss or discomfort feels most charged
- whether I am missing information or avoiding commitment
```

## Practical Task

For your main decision, list:

- what you gain with each option
- what you lose with each option
- what you are most reluctant to admit

## What to Look For

By the end of Week 2, you should be able to say:

- what the real tradeoffs are
- where emotion is shaping interpretation
- what values are colliding

## Week 3: Move From Analysis to Decision

## Why This Week Matters

Some people do not need more thinking.

They need movement.

This is the week to identify whether:

- more clarity is still needed
- the decision is ready
- the next step is obvious but uncomfortable

The goal of Week 3 is to stop circling and move toward decision or toward the next truly useful action.

## What to Do

Use the clone to identify:

- whether more information is needed
- what question must be answered next
- whether the user is ready to decide
- what action would move things forward

## Prompt 1: Next Useful Move

```text
Act as my decision guide.

Help me identify the next useful move in this decision process.

Please tell me:
- whether I need more information, more courage, or more clarity
- what question I still need to answer
- what next conversation, action, or choice would move this forward
```

## Prompt 2: Decision Readiness

```text
Act as my decision support assistant.

Review the material below and help me assess whether I am ready to decide.

Please show:
- what seems clear
- what is still unresolved
- whether the remaining uncertainty is normal or important
- what would make the decision feel actionable
```

## Practical Task

Choose one of the following:

- make the decision
- gather one specific missing piece of information
- have one necessary conversation
- set a clear deadline for deciding

Do not stay abstract this week.

## What to Look For

By the end of Week 3, you should be able to say:

- what the next move is
- why it matters
- what has kept it from happening

## Week 4: Build a Better Decision Rhythm

## Why This Week Matters

The goal is not only to make one better decision.

It is to build a better pattern for future decisions.

This week turns the month into a repeatable practice.

## What to Do

Use the clone to review:

- how you tend to approach decisions
- where you hesitate
- what helped most this month
- what habits would strengthen future decisions

## Prompt 1: Decision Pattern Review

```text
Act as my reflection assistant.

Help me review how I have been making decisions this month.

Please show:
- what patterns helped me
- what patterns delayed me
- what I learned about how I make decisions
- what I should keep, change, or strengthen
```

## Prompt 2: Decision Habit Builder

```text
Act as my planning assistant.

Help me turn what I learned this month into a simple decision rhythm.

Please suggest:
- one weekly decision review habit
- one question I should ask when I feel stuck
- one way to notice avoidance earlier
- one way to move from analysis to action faster
```

## Practical Task

Create a short personal decision rhythm.

Examples:

- weekly unresolved decisions review
- facts vs assumptions check before important choices
- a rule for defining the real decision first
- a deadline rule for medium-stakes decisions

## What to Look For

By the end of Week 4, you should be able to say:

- how your decision process changed
- what support helped most
- what habit you want to keep using

## Common Failure Points

## 1. Treating the Clone Like an Oracle

Problem:

The user starts asking for the “right answer” instead of using the clone to clarify the structure of the decision.

Fix:

Return to framing, tradeoffs, and next-step prompts.

## 2. Staying in Reflection Without Action

Problem:

Insight increases, but movement does not.

Fix:

Every week should end with a concrete next move.

## 3. Overcomplicating the Decision

Problem:

The process becomes intellectually elegant but practically useless.

Fix:

Keep asking:

- What is the actual decision?
- What matters most?
- What happens next?

## 4. Mistaking Delay for Care

Problem:

The user frames indecision as being thoughtful when it is really avoidance.

Fix:

Ask the clone directly whether the issue is lack of information or reluctance to commit.

## Success Criteria

This playbook is working if, by the end of four weeks:

- the user sees decisions more clearly
- fewer decisions stay muddy for too long
- tradeoffs are easier to name
- movement happens sooner
- the clone supports judgment without replacing it

## Final Principle

The best decision playbook does not make the user dependent on a system.

It helps them become more capable of facing uncertainty with structure, honesty, and forward motion.
