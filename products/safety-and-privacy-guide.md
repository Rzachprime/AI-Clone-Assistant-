# Safety and Privacy Guide

## Purpose

This guide helps clients use a personal AI clone responsibly.

The goal is not to make the product feel restrictive.

The goal is to make it trustworthy.

A clone becomes more useful when the user understands:

- what it does well
- what it should not do
- what information should be handled carefully
- where human review is still necessary

## Why This Matters

AI systems can be helpful, but they can also:

- misunderstand context
- sound more certain than they should
- handle sensitive information poorly if used carelessly
- overstep when boundaries are not clearly defined

Good safety design is not a barrier to usability.

It is part of usability.

## The Core Principle

Use the clone where support is helpful.

Keep the human in control where judgment, responsibility, and consequences matter most.

## Part 1: What a Personal AI Clone Does Well

A personal AI clone works best when the task is:

- repetitive
- low-risk
- easy to review
- text-heavy
- structured enough to guide

Good examples:

- planning the day
- organizing tasks
- sorting a weekly list
- drafting routine email replies
- summarizing notes
- preparing a weekly review
- identifying missing information

## Part 2: What a Personal AI Clone Should Not Do Alone

Do not position the clone as a decision-maker in high-stakes situations.

It should not be trusted to independently:

- give legal advice
- give medical advice
- give financial advice
- make safety decisions
- make promises on the user’s behalf
- send messages without review
- interpret emotionally sensitive situations with full confidence
- manage regulated or highly confidential data casually

## Part 3: Four Levels of Risk

This framework helps clients decide when and how to use the clone.

## Level 1: Low Risk

These are strong starting points.

Examples:

- turning notes into a checklist
- organizing errands
- drafting a grocery list
- preparing a first-pass email draft
- creating a weekly review outline

Rule:

Use freely, but still review before acting externally.

## Level 2: Moderate Risk

These tasks are usually acceptable with review and boundaries.

Examples:

- drafting a more important email
- helping compare routine options
- suggesting calendar priorities
- preparing conversation notes

Rule:

Use the clone for support, but verify details and keep final judgment human.

## Level 3: High Risk

These tasks involve meaningful consequences.

Examples:

- financial planning decisions
- emotionally charged relationship conflicts
- job-offer negotiations
- sensitive health questions
- contract language

Rule:

Use only for reflection, framing, or question generation. Do not rely on the clone for the final answer.

## Level 4: Restricted Use

These areas should generally stay outside the clone unless there is a highly controlled and professional context.

Examples:

- crisis situations
- regulated client data
- highly confidential personal records
- emergency response
- legal filings
- medical diagnosis

Rule:

Do not use the clone as a functional substitute here.

## Part 4: Privacy Basics

Clients need a simple way to think about privacy before they start using the clone.

## Ask These Questions First

1. Is this information sensitive?
2. Would I be comfortable if this information were mishandled?
3. Does this include personal, financial, legal, medical, or confidential business information?
4. Does this require a stronger privacy standard than a general AI tool provides?

If the answer is yes, slow down and use a more careful approach.

## Information to Treat Carefully

- passwords
- account credentials
- banking details
- tax records
- legal documents
- medical records
- private client information
- confidential work materials
- highly personal relationship details

## A Good Working Rule

If the information would feel costly, embarrassing, or risky if exposed or misunderstood, do not treat it as casual input.

## Part 5: Human Review Rules

Every clone system should include clear human review rules.

## Always Review Before:

- sending an email
- confirming a meeting
- giving advice to another person
- committing money
- making a promise
- making a sensitive judgment
- sharing a factual summary externally

## Review More Carefully When:

- dates matter
- tone matters
- stakes are emotional
- the information is private
- the output sounds unusually confident

## Part 6: Common Failure Modes

Clients should know what can go wrong.

## 1. Confident Inaccuracy

The clone may state something clearly even when it is incomplete or wrong.

What to do:

- verify facts
- verify names and dates
- do not assume confidence equals correctness

## 2. Context Collapse

The clone may miss social nuance, hidden priorities, or the emotional meaning of a situation.

What to do:

- add more context
- narrow the task
- keep the clone in a support role

## 3. Boundary Drift

The clone may start doing more than the user originally intended.

What to do:

- state boundaries directly
- repeat review requirements
- define what it must never do

## 4. Over-Automation

The system may become more complicated than the problem it is trying to solve.

What to do:

- simplify
- reduce scope
- return to one clear use case

## Part 7: Safety Rules for Agentic Workflows

As the product family expands into agentic workflows, safety becomes even more important.

An agentic workflow can carry work forward with less prompting, so the system needs more structure.

## Before a Workflow Becomes Agentic, Define:

- the goal
- the trigger
- the allowed actions
- the forbidden actions
- the review point
- the escalation point
- the stop condition

## Example

Safer:

`Every Friday, prepare a weekly review draft from my task list and calendar. Do not send anything. Flag missing information and ask for approval before recommending rescheduling.`

Less safe:

`Manage my week for me automatically.`

## Part 8: The Red-Flag Checklist

Pause before using the clone if any of these are true:

- I am upset and looking for a final answer fast
- the task involves money, law, or health
- I have not defined what the clone should not do
- the information is highly private
- I am tempted not to review the output
- I want the clone to act externally without approval

If two or more are true, step back and reduce the scope.

## Part 10: Oversight Is a Feature

Some users may feel that review and boundaries make the system less powerful.

The opposite is closer to the truth.

A clone becomes more useful over time when:

- the user trusts it
- the use cases are clear
- the outputs are reviewed
- the system earns more responsibility gradually

This is the right path toward agentic workflows.

Not maximum autonomy first.

Progressive trust first.

## Final Principle

The safest clone is not the one that does the least.

It is the one that does the right amount, in the right areas, with the right boundaries.
