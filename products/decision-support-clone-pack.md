# Decision Support Clone Pack

## Purpose

This pack helps clients use a personal AI clone to think through decisions with more clarity, structure, and follow-through.

It is not designed to make decisions for the user.

It is designed to help the user think better before deciding.

That means helping with:

- framing
- tradeoffs
- missing information
- priorities
- next steps

## Why This Matters

Many decisions do not fail because the user is incapable.

They fail because the decision process is crowded by:

- too many inputs
- too little clarity
- emotional noise
- unclear tradeoffs
- avoidance
- endless rethinking

A personal AI clone can be useful here when it serves as a decision support system rather than a false authority.

## What This Pack Is For

This pack is useful for:

- sorting through options
- clarifying what the real decision is
- identifying tradeoffs
- separating facts from assumptions
- preparing for a decision conversation
- deciding what needs to be answered next
- reviewing whether the user is avoiding a decision

## What This Pack Is Not For

This pack is not designed to:

- make legal decisions
- provide financial advice
- provide medical advice
- override the user’s values
- create false certainty
- replace judgment in high-stakes situations

The clone supports the process.

The human still owns the decision.

## The Core Promise

The Decision Support Clone should help the user:

- see the real decision more clearly
- reduce noise
- identify what matters most
- weigh options more honestly
- move toward a decision instead of circling endlessly

## What a Good Decision Support Clone Actually Does

A strong version of this clone should be able to:

- define the decision in plain language
- identify the key options
- show likely tradeoffs
- separate known facts from open questions
- identify risks and consequences
- surface value conflicts
- suggest the next question to answer

That is enough to make the clone genuinely useful.

## Where This Clone Helps Most

This clone is often most useful when the user says things like:

- “I know I need to decide, but I keep going in circles.”
- “I cannot tell what matters most here.”
- “There are too many factors.”
- “I am not sure what I am actually choosing between.”
- “I need help thinking this through clearly.”

That is the right lane for this pack.

## The First Principle

Good decision support starts by asking:

`What is the actual decision here?`

Many people believe they have a decision problem when they really have:

- a clarity problem
- a values conflict
- an information gap
- a fear of consequence

A good decision clone helps separate those.

## Decision Support Workflow

This pack works best as a sequence rather than a single prompt.

## Step 1: Frame the Decision

The user provides:

- the situation
- the options as they currently see them
- why the choice feels difficult

The clone should help answer:

- what is the actual decision?
- what are the real options?
- is this one decision or several smaller ones?

## Step 2: Surface Tradeoffs

The user provides:

- what matters most
- what they fear losing
- what they hope to gain

The clone should help answer:

- what are the core tradeoffs?
- what values are in tension?
- what seems emotionally loud but strategically weak?

## Step 3: Separate Facts From Assumptions

The user provides:

- what they believe is true
- what they are worried might happen

The clone should help sort:

- known facts
- assumptions
- predictions
- unknowns

## Step 4: Identify the Next Clarifying Move

The user provides:

- what still feels unresolved

The clone should help identify:

- the next question to answer
- the next conversation to have
- the next piece of information to gather
- whether the user is ready to decide now

## A Simple Decision Support Prompt

```text
Act as my decision support assistant.

Help me think through this decision without making it for me.

Please:
- clarify what the real decision is
- identify the main options
- surface the most important tradeoffs
- separate facts from assumptions
- show me what question I should answer next

Keep the response practical, clear, and grounded.
Do not pretend there is certainty where there is not.
```

## Prompt Variations for Common Decision Situations

## A. Basic Decision Framing Prompt

```text
Act as my decision support assistant.

I need help framing a decision clearly.

Based on the notes below:
- tell me what the actual decision is
- identify the real options
- point out whether I am combining multiple decisions into one

Keep the answer simple and structured.
```

## B. Tradeoff Prompt

```text
Act as my decision support assistant.

Help me understand the tradeoffs in this decision.

Please show:
- what I gain with each option
- what I risk with each option
- what value or priority is in tension
- what seems most likely to matter in six months
```

## C. Facts vs Assumptions Prompt

```text
Act as my clarity assistant.

Review the decision notes below and sort them into:
- known facts
- assumptions
- fears
- unknowns

If something is not clearly supported, do not label it as fact.
```

## D. Next-Step Prompt

```text
Act as my decision guide.

Help me figure out the next useful move in this decision process.

Please tell me:
- what still needs clarification
- whether I need more information or more courage
- what conversation, question, or action would move this forward
```

## E. Decision Debrief Prompt

```text
Act as my reflection assistant.

I made a decision and want to review how I made it.

Help me assess:
- what I did well
- where I hesitated
- what I avoided
- what I should learn for the next important decision
```

- Next step

## Format 2: Option Comparison

- Option A

- facts

- unknowns
- what to answer next

## Common Failure Points

## 1. Asking the Clone to Decide

Problem:

The user slips from decision support into decision outsourcing.

Fix:

Restate that the clone is there to clarify, compare, and surface tradeoffs, not choose on the user’s behalf.

## 2. Starting With the Wrong Question

Problem:

The user asks which option is best before defining the actual decision.

Fix:

Begin with framing.

## 3. Confusing Emotion With Evidence

Problem:

The user treats a fear or impression as a fact.

Fix:

Use the facts-vs-assumptions prompt before moving forward.

## 4. Looking for Certainty That Does Not Exist

Problem:

The user wants the clone to remove ambiguity entirely.

Fix:

Ask the clone to identify the best next move, not a perfect answer.

## 5. Staying in Analysis Too Long

Problem:

The clone helps the user think, but the user never moves toward action.

Fix:

Always ask for a next question, next conversation, or next step.

## A Decision Review Checklist

Before acting on the output, the user should ask:

1. Did the clone clarify the real decision?
2. Did it separate facts from assumptions?
3. Did it reveal a tradeoff I was ignoring?
4. Did it help me move forward?
5. Did it overstate certainty?

If `5` is yes, the output needs more careful review.

## Good Use Cases

This clone is well suited for decisions like:

- whether to take on a new project
- how to prioritize competing obligations
- whether to delay, delegate, or drop work
- how to prepare for a difficult but non-crisis conversation
- how to choose between a few realistic next steps

## Caution Areas

Use extra care when the decision involves:

- legal exposure
- health consequences
- financial risk
- emotionally volatile relationship dynamics
- confidential business matters

In those areas, use the clone only for structure and question generation.

## How This Connects to Agentic Workflows

Most decision support should remain prompt-based.

That is usually the right design.

However, a few low-risk workflow patterns can still be useful.

Examples:

- a weekly decision review
- a monthly tradeoff audit
- a recurring “what is unresolved?” reflection

A safe example:

`Every Friday, help me identify any unresolved decisions from the week, sort them by urgency and importance, and show me what question I need to answer next. Do not recommend commitments or external actions without review.`

That is a bounded workflow.

Not a replacement for judgment.

## Success Criteria

The Decision Support Clone is working if:

- the user sees the real decision more clearly
- choices feel less noisy
- tradeoffs become easier to name
- the user moves from circling to action more often
- the clone helps thinking without pretending to remove responsibility

## Final Principle

The best decision support clone does not make the user dependent on it.

It helps the user think clearly enough to decide with more honesty, more structure, and more confidence.
